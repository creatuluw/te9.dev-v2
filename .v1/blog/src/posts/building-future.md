Ever looked at code you wrote six months ago and thought, "What was I thinking?" Yeah, I've been there too. The thing is, the software we build today will be maintained by someone tomorrow. And that "someone" is often future you, scratching your head at past decisions.

So how do we build things that last? Let me share some thoughts.

## Think Beyond the Feature Request

When you're writing code, it's tempting to just ship the feature and move on. I get it. But taking a moment to think about the future can save you a lot of headaches.

Here's what I try to do:

- **Document your decisions** — Future you will thank present you. Trust me on this one.
- **Choose boring technology** — Proven solutions over shiny new tools. Exciting tech is fun, but reliable tech ships.
- **Design for change** — Architect systems that can evolve. Because requirements always change.

## The Truth About Technical Debt

Every shortcut you take today? It accrues interest. That quick fix might seem harmless now, but it can become a real obstacle later.

Here's my approach to managing debt:

1. **Identify debt early** — Call it out when you see it
2. **Make intentional trade-offs** — Sometimes shortcuts are necessary. Just be honest about it
3. **Schedule regular "debt payments"** — Refactoring isn't glamorous, but your future self will appreciate it

## Design for Scale (Even When You're Small)

You might be small today, but why not design with growth in mind?

I focus on these three things:

- **Modular architecture** — Keep things separated and focused
- **Clear interfaces** — Make it obvious how components talk to each other
- **Comprehensive testing** — Because nothing catches bugs like good tests

## The Golden Rule

Here's the thing: the best code is code that's easy to delete and replace. When better solutions emerge (and they will), you want to be able to adapt without rewriting everything.

Build with tomorrow in mind. Future you will be grateful.

Happy coding!
